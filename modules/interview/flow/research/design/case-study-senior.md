# Design / UX — Case Study Interview — Senior Level (7+ years / Design Lead / Director)

## Topic Sequence (typical order)
1. **Design Strategy for a Product Line** — "You're the new Head of Design for our e-commerce platform (3 products, 200M users). Develop a 12-month design strategy." Tests strategic thinking at scale: product audit, user research agenda, design system unification, quality benchmarks, team structure, and OKR definition tied to business goals.
2. **Design System Governance Challenge** — "Your company has 3 product teams with diverging design patterns. Users are confused by inconsistency. How do you unify without slowing teams down?" Tests change management, governance model design, migration strategy, adoption incentives, and balancing team autonomy with brand coherence.
3. **Org-Level Design Challenge** — "The CEO wants to launch in 3 new international markets in 6 months. What's your design approach?" Tests localization strategy, scalable design systems, cultural research planning, team resourcing, quality standards across markets, and realistic timeline negotiation.
4. **Design Deep Dive Presentation** — "Present your most complex, highest-impact project." Unlike mid-level case studies, senior presentations are evaluated on strategic framing, org-level impact, cross-functional leadership, and business outcome quantification — not just design craft.
5. **Turnaround Scenario** — "You inherited a product with declining NPS (dropped from 45 to 28 over 12 months). What's your approach?" Tests diagnostic thinking: research strategy, root cause analysis, prioritization framework, quick wins vs. structural fixes, and stakeholder alignment on the recovery plan.
6. **Design Culture & Quality at Scale** — "You're building a design team from 5 to 25 designers in 18 months. How do you maintain quality?" Tests hiring philosophy, onboarding design, critique culture establishment, quality metrics, career ladder design, and mentorship infrastructure.

## Phase Structure
| Phase | Duration | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Context & Stakeholder Analysis | 3-5 min | Business context, org structure, user segments, strategic priorities. Senior candidates ask pointed questions about reporting structure, executive alignment, and existing team capabilities. |
| Diagnostic & Research Strategy | 5-8 min | What do you need to learn? Stakeholder interviews, design audit, user research plan, competitive analysis. Not surface-level — specific questions you'd ask, data you'd analyze. |
| Strategic Framework | 8-12 min | Design vision, phased roadmap (30/60/90 day or quarterly), OKRs with design-specific metrics, team topology decisions, design system strategy, and resource plan. |
| Execution & Governance | 5-8 min | How you'd implement: critique cadence, design review gates, handoff standards, design system contribution model, cross-team alignment rituals. |
| Measurement & Accountability | 3-5 min | Multi-tiered metrics: business tier (revenue, LTV, churn), experience tier (SUS, NPS, task success), operational tier (cycle time, component adoption). How you report to executives. |
| Present & Defend | 5-8 min | Cohesive strategic narrative. Expect hard pushback: "That's too slow," "We can't afford that headcount," "How do you justify the ROI?" |

## What Makes This Level Unique
- **Strategic framing is the entire evaluation.** At director level, the case study tests how you think about design as a business function — not how you design a feature. The "case" is an organizational design challenge, not a product design challenge.
- **Senior case studies must sparkle.** Hiring managers at this level expect a project that demonstrates undeniable impact. A complex, relevant project with quantified business outcomes. Being senior means you have more to prove, not less.
- **Business fluency under pressure.** Expect curveball questions framed in business terms: "What's the ROI?", "How does this affect our margin?", "The board wants to see results in Q2." Must translate design value into financial and strategic language.
- **Org design is a design problem.** Team structure, reporting lines, centralized vs. embedded, designer-to-PM ratios, research resourcing — these are design decisions with the same rigor as product decisions.
- **Change management is core.** Every senior design exercise involves changing how an org works. Must demonstrate empathy for existing teams, phased rollout of changes, buy-in strategies, and realistic timelines.
- **Quality systems, not quality heroics.** Strong candidates describe scalable systems (design reviews, quality rubrics, automated checks) rather than personally reviewing every design. The system works without them.
- **C-suite communication is evaluated.** How do you present to executives who don't speak design? Must translate NPS into revenue, SUS into productivity, and design system investment into developer velocity.

## Common Exercises
- Design strategy for a multi-product company entering new markets
- Design system unification across acquired or divergent products
- NPS/quality turnaround for a struggling product
- Build and scale a design team (hiring plan, structure, culture)
- Design ops implementation for a design-immature organization
- Design vision presentation to a simulated executive audience
- Take-home: 1-week strategic design proposal with executive presentation

## Anti-Patterns
- Treating the exercise as an IC design task (wireframing specific features)
- Proposing strategy without connecting to business metrics
- Ignoring existing team capabilities and political realities
- Over-indexing on design system details at the expense of strategic vision
- Presenting a plan without clear milestones and accountability mechanisms
- Avoiding the hard questions: headcount, timeline, budget, tradeoffs
- Generic "design thinking" platitudes without concrete actions
- Proposing changes without a change management strategy
- Focusing only on craft quality without addressing velocity and scale
- Not demonstrating executive communication skills

## Probe Patterns
- ROI pressure: "The CFO asks why we're spending $2M on a design system rebuild. What's your pitch?"
- Timeline challenge: "The board wants this in 6 months, not 12. What do you cut?"
- Team resistance: "The engineering VP says embedded designers slow down sprints. How do you respond?"
- Quality vs. speed: "Product wants to ship weekly. Design reviews take 3 days. How do you resolve this?"
- Metrics depth: "You say design improved retention — how do you isolate design's contribution from product and marketing changes?"
- Stakeholder conflict: "The CPO wants feature velocity. The CDO wants design excellence. You report to both. How do you navigate?"
- Market complexity: "Expansion into Japan requires significant cultural adaptation. How does your design system handle this without fragmenting?"
- Failure scenario: "Six months in, your strategy isn't hitting OKRs. What do you do?"
- Hiring philosophy: "You need 10 designers. Do you hire senior ICs, mid-level, or a mix? Why?"
