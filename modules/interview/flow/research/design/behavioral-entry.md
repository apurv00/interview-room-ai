# Design / UX — Behavioral Interview — Entry Level (0-2 years)

## Topic Sequence (typical order)
1. **Portfolio Walkthrough & Design Motivation** — "Walk me through your favorite project and why you chose it." Tests whether candidate can articulate design decisions and shows genuine passion for user-centered design.
2. **Design Process & Fundamentals** — "Describe your design process from research to delivery." Entry-level candidates should demonstrate awareness of the full UCD cycle (research, ideation, prototyping, testing, iteration) even if experience is limited.
3. **Handling Design Feedback** — "Tell me about a time you received critical feedback on your design. How did you respond?" Tests ego resilience and growth mindset — essential for junior designers who will receive constant critique.
4. **Collaboration with Developers** — "Tell me about working with engineers on a design handoff." Tests communication across disciplines, understanding of implementation constraints, and ability to spec designs clearly.
5. **User Empathy & Advocacy** — "Tell me about a time you discovered something surprising from user research." Tests whether candidate instinctively centers decisions on user needs rather than personal aesthetic preference.
6. **Working Under Constraints** — "Tell me about designing with significant limitations (time, budget, tech)." Juniors often face tight constraints — tests resourcefulness and pragmatic design thinking.
7. **Learning from Failure** — "Tell me about a design that didn't work out as expected." Tests self-awareness, ability to identify what went wrong, and willingness to iterate rather than defend a flawed solution.
8. **Initiative & Curiosity** — "Tell me about a project you pursued on your own." Side projects, redesign concepts, design challenges, hackathons — tests intrinsic motivation and proactive learning.

## Phase Structure
- **Warm-Up (5 min):** "Tell me about yourself" — background, how they got into design, what excites them about UX. Assess communication clarity.
- **Portfolio Context (5-8 min):** Walkthrough of 1-2 projects. Not a full portfolio review — behavioral lens on decisions, collaboration, and learning.
- **Core Behavioral (20-25 min):** 3-4 deep questions on feedback handling, collaboration, user empathy, constraints. 6-7 min each with probes. Go deep on process thinking.
- **Candidate Questions (5 min):** Quality of questions evaluated — curiosity about design culture, team structure, design system, user research practices.

## What Makes This Level Unique
- **Process over polish.** Interviewers evaluate thinking clarity and structured approach, not pixel-perfect deliverables. A rough wireframe with clear rationale beats a beautiful mockup with no reasoning.
- **Non-professional stories expected and fine.** Bootcamp projects, school work, volunteer redesigns, self-initiated case studies, design challenges (Daily UI, etc.) all acceptable.
- **Fundamentals are the differentiator.** Knowledge of hierarchy, layout, typography, color theory, and basic accessibility. Strong candidates reference design principles by name.
- **Willingness to learn matters more than expertise.** How do they respond to feedback? Do they seek critique? Do they iterate?
- **User-centricity separates the pack.** Strongest entry-level candidates center stories on user needs, not tools or visual trends.
- **Tool proficiency is table stakes, not the story.** Figma, Sketch, Adobe XD competency expected — but interviewer wants to hear why you designed something, not how you used the tool.

## Anti-Patterns (do NOT ask at this level)
- Design system governance or org-wide design strategy
- Managing stakeholder conflicts with VPs or C-suite
- Leading or mentoring other designers
- Design metrics (NPS, SUS, task success rates) at portfolio level
- Cross-product design consistency or brand system decisions
- Hiring, team structure, or design operations
- Expecting quantified business outcomes from projects

## Probe Patterns
- Process clarity: "You showed the final design — what did it look like before? What changed and why?"
- User evidence: "How did you know this was the right solution for users?"
- Feedback resilience: "What was the hardest piece of feedback you received? What did you do with it?"
- Collaboration depth: "What did the developer say when you handed off? Did anything change?"
- Ownership isolation: "You said 'we designed' — what specifically did YOU do versus the team?"
- Self-awareness: "If you could redo this project, what would you change?"
