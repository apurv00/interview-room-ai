# Design / UX — Case Study Interview — Mid Level (3-6 years)

## Topic Sequence (typical order)
1. **Design a Product End-to-End** — "Design a meal planning app for busy parents" or "Design a peer mentoring platform for a university." Full-scope exercise: user research plan, problem framing, information architecture, key flows, interaction patterns, and success metrics. Tests structured thinking across the entire design process, not just UI.
2. **Redesign a Major Experience** — "Redesign the onboarding experience for [company's product]" or "Redesign how users discover content on a streaming platform." Tests ability to evaluate existing design, identify problems with evidence, and propose a coherent redesign strategy — not just cosmetic changes.
3. **Design Sprint Simulation** — "You have 45 minutes. Take this problem from research to testable prototype." Compressed design sprint: clarify scope, sketch user journey, define key screens, create a low-fidelity prototype concept, and define a usability test plan. Tests time management and structured process under pressure.
4. **App Critique + Improvement** — "Here's [real app]. Evaluate the experience and redesign the weakest flow." Meta and Google use this format extensively. Tests critical analysis (heuristics, accessibility, IA, interaction patterns) and constructive redesign proposal with rationale.
5. **Multi-User / Multi-Sided Design** — "Design a platform connecting freelance designers with small businesses." Tests ability to design for multiple user types with competing needs, consider both sides of the marketplace, and balance simplicity with feature depth.
6. **Data-Informed Design** — "Here's usage data showing 40% drop-off at step 3 of the signup flow. Diagnose and redesign." Tests analytical thinking: interpreting funnel data, hypothesizing causes, designing experiments (A/B tests), and proposing solutions grounded in evidence rather than assumption.

## Phase Structure
| Phase | Duration | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Clarify & Scope | 3-5 min | Business context, target users, constraints, success criteria. Define what's in/out of scope. Strong candidates negotiate scope. |
| Research Strategy | 3-5 min | What research would you do? Who would you talk to? What existing data would you look at? Not actually doing research — demonstrating research thinking. |
| Problem Framing | 4-5 min | User needs, jobs-to-be-done, journey map of current experience, key pain points. Prioritize top 2-3 problems to solve. |
| Information Architecture | 3-4 min | Content hierarchy, navigation model, key user flows. Card sort reasoning, mental model alignment. |
| Design Solution | 8-12 min | Key screens with annotations. Interaction patterns, state design, responsive considerations. Narrate decisions and tradeoffs. |
| Usability & Metrics | 3-5 min | How would you test this? What metrics would you track? A/B test design. Definition of success. |
| Present & Defend | 3-5 min | Cohesive walkthrough connecting research to decisions. Handle interviewer pushback with reasoning, not defensiveness. |

## What Makes This Level Unique
- **End-to-end ownership expected.** Unlike entry-level (single feature), mid-level exercises span the full product or experience. Must demonstrate research thinking, information architecture, interaction design, and measurement — not just screen layout.
- **Problem framing is the differentiator.** Boring mid-level answers jump to solutions. Strong answers spend time understanding the problem space, defining user segments, and prioritizing which problems to solve. The best candidates reframe the prompt.
- **Case study narrative matters.** At FAANG companies, the case study presentation is the primary evaluation mechanism. How you tell the story — problem framing, research insights, design rationale, measured impact, lessons learned — matters as much as the design itself.
- **Quantitative reasoning required.** "This could affect ~30% of daily active users" with rough reasoning. Must connect design decisions to measurable outcomes and propose experiment designs.
- **Multi-stakeholder awareness.** Consider engineering feasibility, business model implications, content strategy, and accessibility — not just user desirability.
- **Tradeoff articulation separates strong from adequate.** The best answer isn't the "right" solution — it's the clearest tradeoff analysis: "I chose X over Y because Z, accepting the cost of W."
- **Time management is a meta-skill being tested.** Spending 30 minutes on research and 5 on design is as bad as skipping research entirely. Demonstrate prioritization under time pressure.

## Common Exercises
- Design a new product for a specific audience and context
- Redesign a major flow of a real product (onboarding, search, checkout, discovery)
- App critique followed by redesign of the weakest area
- Data-driven diagnosis: given analytics data, identify problems and design solutions
- Multi-sided platform design (marketplace, social, collaboration tool)
- Take-home exercise: 3-5 day design challenge with presentation

## Anti-Patterns
- Skipping research/problem framing and jumping to screens
- Designing only the happy path (no error, empty, loading, edge states)
- Generic solutions disconnected from the specific user context
- No success metrics or measurement strategy
- Ignoring business model or engineering feasibility
- Over-polishing visuals at the expense of interaction logic
- Presenting a single solution without explaining alternatives considered
- Not engaging the interviewer — treating it as a solo performance
- Spending all time on IA without showing any concrete UI
- Using frameworks (Design Thinking, Double Diamond) as buzzwords without applying them

## Probe Patterns
- Scope negotiation: "That's ambitious for 45 minutes. What would you cut to focus deeper?"
- Research depth: "You said you'd do user interviews — what questions? How many? Why?"
- Tradeoff pressure: "Engineering says this will take 3 months. Get 80% of the value in 4 weeks — what do you ship first?"
- Metric challenge: "You said you'd measure 'engagement' — define that precisely. What goes up? What's the guardrail?"
- Stakeholder curveball: "The VP of Marketing wants a promotional banner on this screen. How do you respond?"
- Accessibility push: "How does this work for a user who only uses keyboard navigation?"
- Competitive context: "A competitor just launched something similar. What's your differentiation?"
- Data interpretation: "The data shows high engagement but low retention. What does that tell you about the design?"
