# Design / UX — Behavioral Interview — Mid Level (3-6 years)

## Topic Sequence (typical order)
1. **Design Leadership on a Project** — "Tell me about a project where you drove the design direction end-to-end." Tests ownership, strategic framing, and ability to lead design within a cross-functional team without being the manager.
2. **Stakeholder Conflict & Persuasion** — "Tell me about a time a PM or executive disagreed with your design recommendation. How did you handle it?" FAANG staple — tests ability to advocate with data, user evidence, and business reasoning rather than opinion.
3. **User Advocacy Under Pressure** — "Tell me about championing a user need when the team wanted to cut it for speed." Tests conviction, communication of user value, and pragmatic compromise — not stubbornness.
4. **Cross-Functional Influence** — "How do you build alignment between design, product, and engineering when priorities conflict?" Tests understanding of each function's concerns and ability to find shared ground.
5. **Design Critique & Giving Feedback** — "Tell me about giving difficult feedback to another designer." Mid-level designers participate in and lead design critiques — tests constructiveness, specificity, and psychological safety awareness.
6. **Navigating Ambiguity in Design** — "Tell me about a project where the problem wasn't well-defined. How did you scope it?" Tests ability to frame problems independently, conduct discovery research, and define success criteria.
7. **Measuring Design Impact** — "How have you measured the success of your design work?" Tests analytical thinking — using metrics (task success rate, NPS, conversion, error rate) to validate design decisions, not just qualitative satisfaction.
8. **Mentorship & Team Contribution** — "How have you helped less experienced designers grow?" Tests investment in team quality, design culture contributions (critique sessions, shared learnings, onboarding new designers).

## Phase Structure
- **Warm-Up (3-4 min):** Brief background — current role, team structure, scope of design responsibility. Assess how they frame their impact.
- **Core Behavioral (30-35 min):** 4-5 deep questions on influence, stakeholder management, user advocacy, ambiguity, impact measurement. 7-8 min each with probes. Expect concrete examples with outcomes.
- **Design Culture Contribution (5-7 min):** How do they elevate the team? Critique participation, process improvements, knowledge sharing.
- **Candidate Questions (5 min):** Strong candidates ask about design org maturity, research resources, designer-to-PM ratio, career growth paths.

## What Makes This Level Unique
- **Influence without authority is the core signal.** Must demonstrate persuading PMs, engineers, and leadership through user evidence and data — not just "I designed it and they built it."
- **Expected to own the problem, not just the pixels.** Should independently scope design work, conduct or commission research, and connect designs to product outcomes.
- **Stories must have measurable outcomes.** "The redesign improved X by Y%" or "Usability testing showed Z." Qualitative-only answers are weak at this level.
- **Stakeholder fluency required.** Must speak the language of PMs (metrics, roadmaps), engineers (feasibility, tech debt), and leadership (business impact, strategic fit).
- **Design critique leadership expected.** Not just receiving feedback well — actively giving structured, actionable feedback to peers.
- **Portfolio case studies are behavioral evidence.** Interviewers may reference portfolio work and ask behavioral probes: "You showed this project — tell me about the hardest stakeholder conversation during it."

## Anti-Patterns (do NOT ask at this level)
- Building a design org from scratch or defining org-wide design vision
- C-suite executive persuasion or board-level design strategy
- Hiring and managing a team of designers (they may contribute, but don't own)
- Design operations or tooling decisions at org scale
- Multi-product design system governance
- Budget ownership or vendor management

## Probe Patterns
- Influence evidence: "You say you convinced the PM — walk me through exactly how. What data did you use?"
- Stakeholder empathy: "What was the PM's concern? Why were they pushing back?"
- Impact specificity: "You said the redesign was successful — what metric moved? By how much?"
- Tradeoff awareness: "What did you sacrifice in that design to meet the timeline?"
- Research rigor: "How many users did you test with? Why that number? What was your screening criteria?"
- Critique depth: "Give me an example of specific feedback you gave another designer that changed their approach."
- Failure ownership: "Tell me about a design decision you made that you'd reverse today."
