# Design / UX — Technical Interview — Mid Level (3-6 years)

## Topic Sequence (typical order)
1. **Portfolio Deep Dive — Case Study Presentation** — "Present your strongest project end-to-end. I'll interrupt with questions." 15-20 min deep dive into 1-2 projects. Interviewers probe research methodology, design rationale, iteration cycles, stakeholder alignment, and measurable outcomes. At mid-level, the case study IS the technical interview.
2. **Design Systems** — "How have you contributed to or built a design system? Walk me through your approach to component architecture." Tests understanding of atomic design, token systems, component APIs, versioning, documentation, and adoption strategies. Mid-level should be a contributor, not just a consumer.
3. **Advanced User Research Methods** — "You mentioned usability testing — walk me through your study plan: recruitment, screening, task design, analysis." Tests depth beyond basic methods: moderated vs. unmoderated, synthesis techniques, affinity mapping, journey mapping, competitive analysis, and how insights translate to design decisions.
4. **Accessibility Expertise** — "How do you incorporate accessibility throughout your design process, not just at the end?" Tests WCAG 2.1 AA knowledge in practice: semantic structure, focus management, ARIA patterns, motion sensitivity, screen reader testing. Expected to design accessibly from the start, not retrofit.
5. **App Critique / Design Teardown** — "Here's [app] — evaluate its UX. What's working? What would you redesign and why?" Tests critical analysis using heuristics, understanding of interaction patterns, awareness of business constraints, and ability to propose improvements with reasoning. Commonly a real FAANG product.
6. **Advanced Prototyping & Interaction Design** — "How would you prototype and communicate this complex interaction to engineering?" Tests micro-interaction design, state management (loading, empty, error, success), animation principles (easing, duration, purpose), and fidelity decisions (when lo-fi vs. hi-fi prototyping).
7. **Information Architecture & Navigation** — "This product has 50+ features. How would you organize the navigation?" Tests card sorting knowledge, tree testing, mental model alignment, progressive disclosure, and scalable IA patterns.
8. **Design-to-Development Handoff** — "Walk me through how you hand off designs to engineering." Tests specification quality, design token usage, responsive behavior documentation, interaction specs, and collaboration practices (Figma dev mode, Storybook alignment).
9. **Cross-Platform Design** — "How does your design approach change between mobile and desktop? What about responsive vs. adaptive?" Tests understanding of platform conventions (iOS HIG, Material Design), touch vs. cursor interactions, and responsive design strategy.

## Phase Structure
| Phase | Duration | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Case Study Presentation | 15-20 min | Candidate presents 1-2 projects. Interviewer interrupts with probing questions on research, rationale, tradeoffs, and outcomes. |
| Design Systems & IA Deep Dive | 8-10 min | Component architecture, token systems, navigation patterns, scalability. |
| Accessibility & Standards | 5-8 min | WCAG knowledge, inclusive design practices, testing approaches. |
| App Critique (if applicable) | 10-15 min | Evaluate a real product, propose improvements. Some companies (Meta) dedicate a full round to this. |
| Cross-Functional Collaboration | 5 min | Handoff practices, engineering partnership, specification quality. |

## What Makes This Level Unique
- **Process depth, not just process awareness.** Entry-level knows the UCD steps; mid-level has battle-tested them and can discuss what worked, what failed, and why they adapted their approach for different contexts.
- **Case study presentation is the centerpiece.** FAANG companies (Meta, Google, Amazon) use portfolio deep dives as the primary technical evaluation at this level. The quality of the case study narrative — problem framing, research rigor, design rationale, measured impact — determines the outcome.
- **Design systems contributor expected.** Not just using components — contributing new ones, refactoring existing ones, writing documentation, understanding token architecture and theming.
- **Accessibility is a design skill, not a checklist.** Expected to design with accessibility from wireframe stage: semantic hierarchy, focus order, color independence, motion preferences. Should know WCAG 2.1 AA guidelines and test with screen readers.
- **App critique reveals design maturity.** Strong mid-level candidates evaluate holistically: business context, user segments, information architecture, interaction patterns, visual hierarchy, accessibility — not just "I'd change the color."
- **Quantified impact expected in case studies.** "We improved task completion by 23%" or "Error rate dropped from 12% to 3% after the redesign." Vague "it was well-received" is weak at this level.
- **Research methodology depth.** Should articulate recruitment strategy, sample size reasoning, task design, synthesis approach, and how findings led to specific design changes.

## Anti-Patterns (do NOT ask at this level)
- Basic design vocabulary (what is UX, what is a wireframe, what is a persona)
- Tool-level questions (how to use Figma auto layout — assume proficiency)
- Org-level design strategy or design ops architecture
- Design team hiring, performance management, or career leveling
- Design metrics dashboards or executive reporting frameworks
- Multi-product design system governance at org scale
- Whiteboard coding or technical implementation questions

## Probe Patterns
- Research rigor: "How many participants? How did you recruit them? What was your screening criteria?"
- Design system depth: "How do you handle a component request that doesn't fit the existing system?"
- Accessibility practice: "Walk me through how a screen reader user would complete this task in your design."
- Impact measurement: "You said the redesign was successful — what metric moved? How did you isolate design's contribution?"
- Critique depth: "You critiqued the app's onboarding — now sketch a better version and explain your choices."
- Tradeoff articulation: "You had to choose between a more accessible design and a tighter timeline. Walk me through that decision."
- Cross-platform awareness: "How did this design change for mobile? What did you cut and why?"
- Iteration evidence: "Show me version 1 vs. the final. What feedback drove the biggest change?"
