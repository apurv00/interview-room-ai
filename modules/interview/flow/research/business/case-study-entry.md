# Business & Strategy — Case Study Interview — Entry Level (0-2 years / Analyst / Associate)

## Topic Sequence (typical order)
1. **Profitability Decline** — "Our client is a national restaurant chain whose profits dropped 20% this year. Diagnose and fix." THE most common entry-level case type (~30% of all first-round cases at MBB). Tests: Profit = Revenue - Costs decomposition, isolating the driver (price, volume, fixed, variable), then root-cause analysis.
2. **Market Sizing** — "Estimate the annual revenue of all coffee shops in New York City." Tests structured estimation, assumption-making, and mental math. Two approaches: top-down (population → penetration → frequency → spend) or bottom-up (# locations → daily traffic → average ticket).
3. **Market Entry** — "Should a European luxury retailer enter the Indian market?" Tests ability to assess market attractiveness (size, growth, competition), internal capabilities (brand fit, supply chain, capital), and entry strategy (organic, JV, acquisition). ~15-20% of all first-round cases.
4. **Revenue Growth** — "A B2B software company's growth has stalled at $50M ARR. How should they reignite growth?" Tests ability to explore growth levers: new customers, new segments, new products, pricing, geographic expansion, channel strategy.
5. **Pricing Strategy** — "A pharmaceutical company is launching a new drug. How should they price it?" Tests value-based vs. cost-plus vs. competitive pricing frameworks. Must consider willingness-to-pay, competitive alternatives, regulatory constraints.
6. **Cost Reduction** — "A manufacturing company needs to cut costs by 15% without reducing output. Where do you start?" Tests structured cost decomposition (fixed vs. variable, by function, by input) and ability to prioritize cuts by impact and feasibility.
7. **Basic M&A / Should We Acquire** — "Should a regional bank acquire a fintech startup?" Tests ability to evaluate strategic rationale, financial fit, integration complexity, and deal-breaker risks at a conceptual level. Full financial modeling not expected.

## Phase Structure
| Phase | Duration | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Listen & Clarify | 2-3 min | Hear the case prompt. Ask 2-3 clarifying questions: objective, scope, constraints, key metrics. DO NOT start solving yet. |
| Structure & Framework | 3-4 min | Take 60-90 seconds of silence to structure. Present a MECE framework customized to the case — NOT a memorized template. 3-4 branches with clear logic. |
| Analysis & Hypothesis Testing | 12-15 min | Work through the framework branch by branch. Ask for data, interpret charts/tables, do math. Test hypotheses. Interviewer provides data in response to good questions. |
| Math & Quantification | 5-8 min | At least one quantitative exercise: break-even, market sizing, revenue impact. Talk through calculations out loud. Sanity-check results. |
| Synthesis & Recommendation | 2-3 min | "Based on my analysis, I recommend X for three reasons: 1, 2, 3. Key risks are A and B, which I'd mitigate by..." Clear, decisive, structured. |

## What Makes This Level Unique
- **Custom frameworks over memorized templates.** Top candidates build a framework from scratch for each case. Robotically applying "profitability tree" to every case is a red flag. Understand the building blocks, then assemble per context.
- **Profitability is the most common and most testable type.** Revenue = Price x Quantity. Costs = Fixed + Variable. The formula is simple — the skill is knowing which branch to explore first and how to isolate the root cause.
- **Market sizing is evaluated on structure, not precision.** A well-reasoned answer within 2x of reality passes. An exact number with sloppy logic fails.
- **Math must be done out loud.** Silent calculation is the fastest way to fail a case. The interviewer needs to follow your logic, catch errors, and evaluate your thought process.
- **The #1 entry-level mistake: jumping into analysis without structuring first.** Taking 60-90 seconds of silence to organize thinking is not just acceptable — it's expected and rewarded.
- **Synthesis must be decisive.** "It depends" is not a recommendation. Pick a direction, state 2-3 reasons, acknowledge key risks, and propose mitigation.
- **Most cases need 25-35 minutes.** Practice pacing — don't spend 20 minutes on structure and run out of time for math and synthesis.

## Common Problems
- Restaurant chain profitability decline (revenue vs. cost isolation)
- Market sizing: coffee shops, gas stations, smartphones in a city
- European company entering Asian market (market entry)
- Subscription business revenue growth stall
- New product pricing (pharmaceutical, SaaS, consumer)
- Manufacturing cost reduction by X%
- Should we acquire this company? (strategic rationale level)

## Anti-Patterns
- Memorized frameworks applied without adaptation ("let me use my profitability framework")
- Doing math silently — interviewer can't follow reasoning
- Losing zeros in multiplication — always sanity-check large numbers
- Forgetting to ask clarifying questions before structuring
- Analysis without "so what" — stating numbers without interpreting their strategic meaning
- Wishy-washy recommendation: "We could do A or B, it depends on..." — must commit
- Over-engineering the structure and leaving too little time for insights
- Ignoring data the interviewer provides — not incorporating new information into the framework

## Probe Patterns
- "Before you start, what clarifying questions do you have?"
- "You said revenue is the issue. How would you narrow down whether it's price or volume?"
- "That number seems off. Can you walk me through the calculation again?"
- "Good analysis. Now, if you could only investigate one more thing before making a recommendation, what would it be?"
- "The CEO is in 5 minutes. Give me your recommendation in 30 seconds."
- "What's the biggest risk to your recommendation? How would you mitigate it?"
