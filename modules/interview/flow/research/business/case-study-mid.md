# Business & Strategy — Case Study Interview — Mid Level (3-6 years / Senior Associate / Engagement Manager)

## Topic Sequence (typical order)
1. **M&A Strategy & Due Diligence** — "A PE firm is considering acquiring a mid-market logistics company for $300M. Should they proceed?" Tests comprehensive deal evaluation: strategic rationale, financial attractiveness (multiples, DCF), commercial due diligence (market position, customer concentration), operational risks, and synergy quantification. ~20% of mid-level cases.
2. **Market Entry with Full Business Case** — "Should a US health-tech company expand into Germany? Build the business case." Goes deeper than entry-level: regulatory landscape, reimbursement models, competitive dynamics, channel strategy, required investment, 5-year financial projection, and go-to-market timeline.
3. **Growth Strategy & Prioritization** — "A $500M industrial company wants to double revenue in 5 years. Design the growth strategy." Tests ability to evaluate and prioritize multiple growth vectors: organic (new products, segments, geographies) vs. inorganic (acquisitions, partnerships). Must quantify each option's contribution.
4. **Operational Improvement & Turnaround** — "A consumer goods manufacturer's margins are 500bp below industry average. Diagnose and design an improvement program." Tests operational benchmarking, cost decomposition by value chain step, lean/six-sigma thinking, and phased implementation plan with quick wins and structural changes.
5. **Pricing & Revenue Optimization** — "An enterprise SaaS company is leaving money on the table. Redesign the pricing architecture." Tests segmented pricing, value metrics, bundling strategy, competitive positioning, migration planning for existing customers, and revenue impact modeling.
6. **Competitive Response & Defensive Strategy** — "A well-funded startup is disrupting your client's core market with a free product. Design the response." Tests game-theoretic thinking: when to compete, when to acquire, when to partner, when to concede a segment. Must consider second-order effects.
7. **Digital Transformation Business Case** — "A traditional retailer wants to build an e-commerce capability. Build vs. buy vs. partner?" Tests ability to evaluate technology decisions in business context: investment required, time-to-market, competitive urgency, capability gaps, and organizational readiness.
8. **Root Cause Diagnosis** — "Conversion rate dropped 15% this quarter. Diagnose and fix." Tests structured decomposition of a metric decline: funnel analysis, segment analysis, external vs. internal factors, and ability to ask for the right data to narrow hypotheses.
9. **Portfolio Optimization** — "A conglomerate has 8 business units. Which to invest in, hold, or divest?" Tests portfolio-level thinking: growth-share matrix, strategic fit, capital efficiency, and ability to make hard tradeoff recommendations.

## Phase Structure
| Phase | Duration | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Clarify & Scope | 2-3 min | Business context, company stage, strategic objectives, constraints. At mid-level, better scoping questions = better case. |
| Market & Competitive Context | 3-4 min | Competitive landscape, market dynamics, customer segmentation. Must go beyond generic to demonstrate industry awareness. |
| Problem Structuring | 4-5 min | Custom MECE framework with quantitative intuition. "This branch likely accounts for 60% of the impact because..." Not just structure — prioritized structure. |
| Analytical Deep Dive | 10-12 min | Financial modeling, data interpretation, quantitative analysis. Multiple data exhibits. Must integrate findings across exhibits. |
| Synthesis & Recommendation | 3-4 min | Clear recommendation with quantified impact. Risk assessment with probability-weighted scenarios. Implementation timeline. |
| Implementation & Risks | 3-4 min | Go-to-market plan, organizational requirements, key milestones, and what could go wrong. This phase differentiates mid from entry. |

## What Makes This Level Unique
- **Cases are multi-layered and integrate multiple frameworks.** A single case might require market sizing + competitive analysis + financial modeling + organizational design. Entry-level cases test one thing; mid-level cases test integration.
- **Quantitative intuition expected throughout.** "This could affect ~20% of our DAU" with rough reasoning — not just "it's significant." Numbers should flow naturally into the conversation.
- **Implementation is part of the case.** "Good recommendation. Now how do you execute it?" is a standard follow-up. Must address organizational change, stakeholder buy-in, phased rollout, and measurement.
- **Interviewer engagement is expected.** Mid-level cases are conversations, not presentations. Interviewer shares data, challenges assumptions, introduces curveballs. Best candidates adapt in real-time.
- **Industry context matters.** Generic frameworks without industry-specific adaptation fail. Must demonstrate awareness of sector dynamics, regulatory environment, and competitive specifics.
- **Experienced hires face cases based on their industry.** McKinsey/BCG often use cases from the candidate's sector to test whether domain expertise translates to consulting structure.
- **Time pressure is real.** Cases run 30-40 minutes with significantly more ground to cover than entry level. Pacing and prioritization are tested.

## Common Problems
- PE acquisition evaluation (logistics, healthcare, tech)
- International market entry with regulatory complexity
- Revenue growth strategy for plateauing company
- Manufacturing operational improvement
- SaaS pricing architecture redesign
- Competitive response to disruptive entrant
- Digital transformation build/buy/partner decision
- Metric decline diagnosis (conversion, retention, NPS)

## Anti-Patterns
- Generic frameworks without adaptation to case context
- Ignoring business model and monetization implications
- Single-metric optimization without considering tradeoffs (conversion without retention/brand)
- No competitive awareness — analyzing in vacuum
- Over-indexing on novelty when obvious solution exists
- Skipping implementation / go-to-market strategy
- Analysis without quantification — "we should probably" instead of "this would generate $XM"
- Presenting findings without connecting them to the recommendation

## Probe Patterns
- "How different would your recommendation be if optimizing for revenue instead of market share?"
- "Engineering says 6 months. Get 80% of value in 6 weeks?"
- "What data would you need before committing to this recommendation?"
- "How does this fit with the company's broader portfolio strategy?"
- "A/B test shows +5% conversion but -2% retention. What do you do?"
- "Walk me through the first 90 days after the board approves your recommendation."
- "The acquiring PE firm expects 3x return in 5 years. Does the deal work?"
