# Business & Strategy — Technical Interview — Entry Level (0-2 years / Analyst / Associate)

## Topic Sequence (typical order)
1. **Market Sizing & Estimation** — "Estimate the size of the electric vehicle market in California." THE entry-level consulting technical question. Tests structured decomposition, comfort with assumptions, and back-of-envelope math. Top-down (population-based) or bottom-up (segment-based) approach expected.
2. **Data Interpretation & Chart Reading** — "Here's a chart of revenue by region over 5 years. What story does this data tell? What would you investigate next?" Tests ability to extract insight from data, not just read numbers. Strongest candidates generate hypotheses unprompted.
3. **Excel / Spreadsheet Proficiency** — "Walk me through how you'd build a pivot table to analyze sales by region and product line." Tests practical tool competency: pivot tables, VLOOKUP/INDEX-MATCH, conditional formatting, basic charts. Intermediate Excel is the baseline.
4. **SQL Fundamentals** — "Write a query to find the top 5 customers by revenue in Q4." Tests basic SQL: SELECT, WHERE, JOIN, GROUP BY, ORDER BY. Window functions are bonus, not expected. Business analyst roles increasingly test SQL directly.
5. **Basic Financial Literacy** — "A company's revenue grew 12% but profit declined 5%. What could explain this?" Tests understanding of revenue vs. cost structure, margin dynamics, and basic P&L logic. Not full financial modeling — conceptual understanding.
6. **Quantitative Reasoning & Mental Math** — "If we raise price by 10% and volume drops 15%, what happens to revenue?" Tests comfort with percentage math, break-even logic, and quick calculations. Talking through math out loud is expected.
7. **Business Framework Application** — "How would you structure an analysis of whether this company should enter the European market?" Tests whether candidate can apply basic frameworks (market attractiveness, competitive landscape, capability fit) without memorized templates.
8. **Data Visualization & Communication** — "How would you present this analysis to a non-technical stakeholder?" Tests ability to translate quantitative findings into clear, actionable narrative. Consulting = communication of analysis, not just analysis.

## Phase Structure
| Phase | Duration | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Warm-Up & Context | 3-5 min | Brief introduction, role context, what to expect in the technical round. |
| Market Sizing / Estimation | 8-10 min | One structured estimation question. Interviewer evaluates approach more than answer. |
| Data Interpretation | 8-10 min | Chart, table, or dataset provided. Extract 2-3 insights and propose next-step analysis. |
| Tool Proficiency (Excel/SQL) | 10-12 min | Hands-on or verbal walkthrough of spreadsheet/query tasks. Some firms use live exercises. |
| Quantitative Reasoning | 5-8 min | Quick-fire math and logic problems. Mental math, percentage calculations, break-even. |
| Wrap-Up | 3-5 min | Questions about the role, team, analytical tools used. |

## What Makes This Level Unique
- **Process over precision.** Interviewers care about how you structure the problem, not whether you get the exact number. A well-reasoned estimate within 2x of the real answer is a pass.
- **Talk through math out loud.** Doing calculations silently is a red flag. The interviewer needs to follow your logic and catch small errors before they compound.
- **The #1 math mistake is losing zeros.** When multiplying large numbers (e.g., 150M x 24), candidates frequently drop or add a zero. Always sanity-check your result against common sense.
- **Excel proficiency is tested at intermediate level.** Pivot tables, VLOOKUP, conditional formatting, basic charts. Advanced VBA or macros are not expected.
- **SQL is increasingly standard.** JOINs, GROUP BY, and basic aggregation are expected even at entry level. Some firms (McKinsey, Deloitte) include live coding exercises.
- **Market sizing uses two approaches:** Top-down (start with total population, narrow by segments) or bottom-up (start with individual unit economics, scale up). Know both.
- **AI/data literacy is a 2025-2026 differentiator.** Firms increasingly ask about how AI tools can enhance business analysis — awareness matters even at junior level.

## Common Problems
- Estimate the number of gas stations in the US
- Estimate the annual revenue of a Starbucks location
- Given a dataset, identify the most profitable customer segment
- Build a simple financial model in Excel for a subscription business
- Write SQL to find monthly revenue trends with year-over-year comparison
- Calculate break-even point given price, variable cost, and fixed costs
- Interpret a declining margin chart and propose root-cause hypotheses

## Anti-Patterns (do NOT ask at this level)
- DCF valuation, WACC calculation, or complex financial modeling
- Advanced statistical methods (regression, hypothesis testing)
- Strategic planning frameworks requiring industry expertise
- M&A analysis, due diligence, or deal structuring
- Multi-scenario financial models with sensitivity analysis
- P&L management or investment thesis development

## Probe Patterns
- Estimation structure: "Walk me through your approach before you start calculating."
- Assumption validation: "That number seems high/low — what assumption is driving that?"
- Sanity check: "Does that answer make sense to you? How would you verify it?"
- Tool depth: "What if the dataset had 100K rows — how would your approach change?"
- Communication: "How would you present this finding to a client who doesn't know Excel?"
- Math verification: "Can you double-check that multiplication? Walk me through it step by step."
