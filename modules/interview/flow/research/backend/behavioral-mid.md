# Backend Engineer — Behavioral Interview — Mid Level (3-6 years)

## Topic Sequence (typical order)
1. **Project ownership narrative** — "Walk me through a significant project you owned end-to-end"
2. **Technical decision-making** — "Tell me about a time you made a major technical decision and how you evaluated the trade-offs"
3. **Conflict resolution with peers** — "Describe a time you had a conflict with a co-worker and how you resolved it"
4. **Leading through blockers** — "Tell me about a project with many technical difficulties and how you overcame them"
5. **Handling ambiguity** — "Describe a situation where requirements were unclear and how you navigated it"
6. **Prioritization under pressure** — "Tell me about a time you had too many things to do and had to prioritize"
7. **Cross-functional collaboration** — "How did you work with PMs, designers, or other teams to deliver a feature?"
8. **Incident response / production issues** — "Describe a production incident you were involved in and how you handled it"
9. **Incident post-mortem walkthrough** — "Walk me through a past production incident: timeline, root cause, and what you changed to prevent recurrence"
10. **Giving and receiving feedback** — "Tell me about a time you gave difficult feedback to a teammate"
11. **Growth and mentoring** — "Have you helped onboard or mentor a more junior engineer?"

## Phase Structure
- **Warm-up (5 min):** Signature project walkthrough. Interviewer calibrates scope expectations.
- **Core competency stories (25-30 min):** 4-5 STAR-format questions covering ownership, conflict, ambiguity, and collaboration. Each response 3-4 minutes with follow-ups.
- **Pressure/failure probe (5-10 min):** At least one "failure" question — interviewers want to see both a win and a fail to evaluate self-reflection.
- **Closing (5 min):** Questions for the interviewer, culture-fit signals, growth trajectory.

## What Makes This Level Unique
- Stories must demonstrate **personal ownership of outcomes** — "I led" not "we did."
- Interviewers assess **initiative**: did you identify problems proactively, or only when assigned?
- **On-call and production incident experience** is now fair game. Interviewers test composure under pressure.
- **Cross-functional collaboration** becomes a core signal — working with product, QA, design, and other engineering teams.
- **Prioritization using frameworks** (urgency/impact matrix, stakeholder negotiation) — not just "I worked hard."
- CARL framework (Context, Actions, Results, Learnings) is a recommended alternative to STAR for experienced candidates, as the Learnings component demonstrates reflective depth. However, STAR remains the most widely used framework.

## Anti-Patterns (do NOT ask at this level)
- Organizational-level strategy (setting engineering-wide direction)
- Building teams from scratch or making hiring decisions
- Influencing company-level roadmap or executive stakeholders
- Setting engineering standards for the entire organization
- Multi-team, multi-quarter impact scope
- Pure academic/theoretical questions (should draw from real production experience)

## Probe Patterns
- **Deep follow-up:** Technical decisions (why that approach, what alternatives, what constraints shaped it), conflict resolution (both perspectives), production incidents (timeline, root cause, postmortem)
- **Moderate depth:** Prioritization (want framework, not just anecdote), cross-functional collaboration (friction points and resolution)
- **Surface treatment:** Self-introduction (calibration only), growth/mentoring (one example suffices)
- **Win-and-fail pattern:** Companies like Meta explicitly ask for both success and failure in the same skill area
